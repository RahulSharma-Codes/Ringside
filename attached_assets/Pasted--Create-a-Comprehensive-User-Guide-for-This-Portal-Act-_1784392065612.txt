# Create a Comprehensive User Guide for This Portal

Act as an expert **Product Documentation Lead, UX Writer, and Visual Designer**.

Your task is to create a **complete, professional, visually appealing user guide for this portal**, based strictly on the functionality that actually exists in the application.

The objective is simple:

> A completely new user should be able to read this guide, understand what the portal does, navigate it confidently, and know how to use every available feature and important workflow.

## 1. Explore the Portal First

Before writing the guide, thoroughly explore the entire portal.

Systematically visit every accessible page, section, tab, menu, view, modal, drawer, settings area, and other relevant interface.

Interact with available features where safe and appropriate to understand how they actually work.

Identify:

* What the portal does
* Who it appears to be designed for
* The main sections and modules
* Every available feature
* The purpose of each feature
* The actions users can perform
* The important workflows connecting different parts of the portal
* Any different views, states, or configurations that users need to understand

Do not rely only on navigation labels. Open features and interact with them sufficiently to understand their actual purpose.

**Only document functionality that you can verify exists.**

Do not invent, assume, or describe features simply because similar applications normally have them.

If something cannot be verified, either omit it or clearly identify it as unverified.

---

## 2. Understand the Portal as a Complete Product

Before documenting individual features, develop a clear understanding of how the portal works as a whole.

Determine:

* The primary purpose of the portal
* The key problems it helps users solve
* The major areas of functionality
* How information flows through the system
* How different sections connect
* The typical journeys a user is likely to follow

Use this understanding to determine the most logical structure for the guide.

Do not force the portal into a predetermined documentation structure. Let the **actual product architecture and user experience determine the structure of the guide**.

---

## 3. Create a Clear Introduction

Begin the guide with a concise overview that explains:

* What the portal is
* What users can accomplish with it
* How the portal is broadly organized
* How users should think about the overall workflow

Keep this section simple enough for a first-time user to understand.

Where useful, include a visual overview, annotated screenshot, or simple workflow diagram to provide an immediate mental model of the product.

---

## 4. Explain Every Major Area of the Portal

Create a dedicated section for each major area or module discovered during your exploration.

For each area, explain:

**What it is**
A simple explanation of the feature or section.

**What it is used for**
Why and when a user would use it.

**What the user sees**
Explain the important information, controls, indicators, and interface elements.

**What the user can do**
Explain all meaningful actions available.

**How to use it**
Provide clear step-by-step instructions for actions that are not immediately obvious.

**What happens next**
Where relevant, explain how actions affect other parts of the portal or connect to subsequent workflows.

Use practical language rather than technical descriptions.

---

## 5. Document Important Workflows

Do not limit the guide to explaining individual screens.

Identify the important end-to-end workflows that actually exist in the portal and explain them from beginning to end.

For each workflow:

1. Explain the objective.
2. Explain where to begin.
3. Walk through each step.
4. Show the relevant screens where helpful.
5. Explain the expected result.
6. Explain what the user can or should do next.

Where useful, create a simple visual flow showing how the steps connect.

Only document workflows that can be verified in the application.

---

## 6. Use Screenshots Intelligently

Capture and include screenshots wherever they materially improve understanding.

Prioritize screenshots for:

* Important screens
* Complex interfaces
* Key workflows
* Features that may not be immediately intuitive
* Actions involving multiple steps

Do not include screenshots simply to increase the number of visuals.

Where useful, annotate screenshots with:

* Numbered callouts
* Highlight boxes
* Arrows
* Short labels

Annotations should help users understand exactly where to look or click.

Use cropped screenshots when a full-screen image would make the relevant feature too small to understand.

Maintain consistent screenshot sizing and annotation styles throughout the guide.

Ensure screenshots are:

* Clear
* Current
* High resolution
* Properly aligned
* Not stretched
* Free from unnecessary browser clutter where possible

Do not expose confidential, sensitive, or personal information in screenshots. Use appropriate safe or demonstration data where available.

---

## 7. Make the Guide Visually Engaging

The final output should not look like a plain technical manual or a large wall of text.

Create a polished, modern enterprise documentation experience.

Use a combination of:

* Clear headings
* Short paragraphs
* Screenshots
* Annotated visuals
* Step-by-step instructions
* Tables where useful
* Process diagrams where useful
* Icons where appropriate
* Callout boxes
* Tips and notes
* Visual separation between sections

Use whitespace thoughtfully.

Keep pages visually balanced and avoid:

* Large unexplained blank spaces
* Overcrowded pages
* Excessive text
* Tiny screenshots
* Repetitive screenshots
* Poorly aligned elements
* Awkward page breaks
* Images separated from their explanations

The visual quality should feel comparable to documentation produced by a high-quality modern enterprise software company.

---

## 8. Write for a First-Time User

Assume the reader has never used the portal before.

Use clear, professional, easy-to-understand language.

Do not assume that users understand what an icon or control does simply because it appears obvious.

At the same time, avoid over-explaining basic actions unnecessarily.

Focus on answering:

> What is this?

> Why would I use it?

> How do I use it?

> What happens when I do this?

> What should I do next?

Keep explanations concise but complete.

---

## 9. Include a Quick Start Section

Create a short **Quick Start** section near the beginning of the guide.

This should allow a new user to understand the essential functionality and begin using the portal without reading the complete guide.

Determine the contents of the Quick Start based on the actual portal and its most important user journeys.

---

## 10. Create Useful Reference Sections

Based on what actually exists in the portal, determine whether the guide would benefit from sections such as:

* Frequently asked questions
* Tips and best practices
* Troubleshooting
* Glossary
* Quick reference

Only include these sections when they add genuine value.

Do not add generic or speculative content merely to make the guide longer.

---

## 11. Make the Guide Easy to Navigate

Structure the document professionally.

Where supported by the output format, include:

* Cover page
* Guide title
* Version or date
* Table of contents
* Clear section numbering
* Consistent headers and footers
* Page numbers
* Clickable internal navigation
* Cross-references between related sections

A user should be able to quickly find instructions for any feature without reading the entire guide.

---

## 12. Verify Completeness

Before finalizing the guide, compare it against everything discovered during the initial portal exploration.

Confirm that:

* Every important section has been covered.
* Every meaningful feature has been explained.
* Every important user action has been documented.
* Major workflows have been covered end-to-end.
* Screenshots correspond to the current interface.
* Instructions accurately reflect actual behavior.
* Terminology matches the terminology used in the portal.
* No functionality has been invented or assumed.

If something was discovered but could not be fully understood or tested, explicitly flag it for review rather than guessing.

---

## 13. Perform a Final Quality Review

Before delivering the final guide, carefully proofread and visually inspect the entire document.

Check for:

* Spelling and grammar errors
* Inconsistent terminology
* Duplicate content
* Missing explanations
* Incorrect instructions
* Broken references
* Poor screenshot quality
* Incorrect annotations
* Image distortion
* Text-image overlaps
* Awkward page breaks
* Excessive whitespace
* Misaligned elements
* Inconsistent formatting

Ensure the guide feels like **one professionally designed document**, not a collection of separately generated sections.

---

# Final Objective

Deliver a **comprehensive, accurate, visually polished user guide based entirely on the portal as it actually exists**.

The guide should serve as the definitive reference for users of the portal.

A new user should finish reading it with a clear understanding of:

1. What the portal does
2. How it is organized
3. What each major feature does
4. How to perform important actions
5. How to complete key workflows
6. Where to find additional functionality when needed

**Explore first. Understand second. Document third. Verify last.**

Do not assume functionality.

Do not invent features.

Do not use generic descriptions where the actual portal can be examined.

Use screenshots when they improve understanding.

Prioritize accuracy, clarity, completeness, and visual quality over unnecessary length.
